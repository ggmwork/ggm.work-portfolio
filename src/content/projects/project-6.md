Project Storm  Art Lead & 3D Artist
Art Direction · 3D Modeling · Real-Time VFX

In 2023, during the final year of my degree in Videogames and Multimedia Design, I did a mandatory curricular internship at Optimizer  a small independent studio developing a puzzle and escape-room experience in Unreal Engine. The demo existed, the mechanics worked, but visually it was bare. Primitive shapes, no art direction, no atmosphere. It wasn't ready to be shown to publishers or pitched for funding, and that's what the internship was there to fix.
The core team was small: two 3D artists, including me, and one sound artist. I started as a contributor  gathering references, building moodboards, proposing visual directions. At some point that shifted, and I found myself leading the artistic direction of the project. It was my first time in a formal leadership role, with real dependencies on other people's work and real consequences if the vision wasn't communicated clearly enough.
Due to NDA restrictions, specific gameplay and narrative details can't be disclosed  but the focus here is on the creative direction, production decisions, and what I learned leading a team for the first time.

The creative direction I pushed for was a hybrid aesthetic  Lovecraftian horror, mysticism, and experimental science layered together. The brief was broad enough to allow it, and it gave the project something most VR escape-room titles don't have: a mood that's immediately distinct. Generic wasn't going to get this demo in front of the right people. A strong, specific aesthetic would.
Before touching any new assets, I did a full audit of everything that existed  cataloguing every placeholder, every primitive, every asset that needed to be redesigned or rebuilt. From that I could prioritize: what had the most visual impact, what was most relevant to gameplay, what could wait. The six game rooms were split between the two of us  I owned Player 1's rooms  which meant maintaining stylistic consistency across work I wasn't directly producing. That required a clear enough visual language that my colleague could apply it independently without constant check-ins.
One of the decisions I'm most satisfied with was building a texture reuse system early. Rather than creating unique textures for every asset, I developed master materials for recurring surfaces  metals, woods, worn finishes  and derived from those. It kept production moving fast without letting visual consistency slip.
All 3D work was done in Blender with baked textures exported directly to Unreal Engine, keeping the pipeline tight and the tool dependency minimal. [Images]

By the end of the internship, all rooms were complete  assets modeled, textured, integrated, interactive logic connected. The demo had a coherent visual identity it didn't have before, and it was in a state where it could genuinely represent the project to publishers and funding opportunities.
But the thing I took away most from this project wasn't technical. Leading a team, even a small one, under real time pressure showed me something I didn't expect: I tended to pull critical tasks back to myself rather than trusting my colleague to execute them. It kept quality where I needed it, but it cost him learning opportunities and cost me the chance to actually lead. I recognized it during the project and I've thought about it since. The difference between managing work and developing people is something I want to keep getting better at.
