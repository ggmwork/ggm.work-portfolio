**NexLed Rebranding & UI Component System***Brand Identity · UI Design · Design Systems*

---

When I joined Tec IT, the gap between what the brands *were* and how they *appeared* was hard to ignore. Tec IT owns several brands  NexLed, NexWatt, EvVision, JG Domótica  and none of them had a consistent visual identity, a defined tone of voice, or any system to keep communications coherent across touchpoints. Content was being created project by project, decision by decision, with nothing to anchor it.

I put together a report analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of each brand and the direction I believed each one should take. When I presented it, my superior's reaction was more measured than I expected  he acknowledged the value but felt a full multi-brand overhaul wasn't the priority. I pushed back, arguing that without defined visual rules every piece of content we produced was starting from zero, and that inconsistency compounds quickly. He recognized it, but held his position: the company's focus was NexLed, and with B2C expansion on the horizon, that's where the effort should go first.

I partially agreed. Doing all brands at once would have allowed us to create distinct personas and a unifying system  the smarter long-term play. But focusing on one meant moving faster, and faster mattered at that moment. So NexLed it was.

---

Before touching anything visual, I built the strategic foundation. Target audience mapping, tone of voice, brand positioning, institutional identity  the documents that would make every visual decision defensible rather than arbitrary. The clearest output of this phase was understanding exactly who NexLed needed to speak to and what made them worth listening to: a company that designs and manufactures custom LED lighting solutions from scratch, built to last, built to perform, with a genuine focus on the client's specific need rather than a catalogue answer.

The insight that drove most of the creative direction came from looking at competitors. LED brands tend to fall into one of two traps  either deeply technical and cold, or aggressively trendy and hollow. NexLed needed to occupy the space between those, speaking fluently to both industry professionals and end consumers without alienating either. Confident enough to be credible, human enough to be approachable, and distinct enough to be remembered.

From that, the brand pillars took shape:

> **Mission**  Develop high-performance, customized LED solutions that combine technology, durability, and sustainability.
**Vision**  Become the Portuguese reference in technical and sustainable lighting, with a growing presence in European and international markets.
**Tone**  Professional and technical, but always accessible. Direct, clear, confident without exaggeration.
**Values**  Quality · Innovation · Efficiency · Customization · Sustainability
**Slogan**  *Light for Every Need*
> 

---

With the strategy defined, the concrete decisions began  and several of them came with real constraints.

The logo was the first. Redesigning it would have required renewing its legal registration in Portugal, which wasn't viable at this stage. So I left the mark itself untouched and instead built a clear set of rules around it  how it relates to the Tec IT brand, how it should and shouldn't be presented, and what contexts it belongs in. A constraint that became a boundary condition for everything else.

The color palette had no existing reference to build from, so I went back to the logo itself. The gradient running through it  red, green, blue  gave me a starting point, and the green tone stood out immediately. None of NexLed's main competitors use green; the category defaults to blue and yellow. Choosing green was both a differentiation move and a meaningful one  it connects naturally to the brand's sustainability and efficiency positioning, and its darker primary shade gives the brand a maturity that lighter, louder palettes don't. Red and blue became the secondary colors, with complementary neutral tones rounding out the system. I also built a set of brighter secondary variants for each primary  for moments where something needs to stand out or feel more alive. *[Images]*

Typography was a deliberate departure from the expected. Most technical brands either use a single typeface or pair two families  and the logical starting point would have been the logo's own font. But it isn't available on Google Fonts or similar platforms, which means importing it manually into every tool: Photoshop, Canva, presentations, web. That's friction at every step, especially as the brand grows into B2C and printed media. I went looking for an alternative and landed on **Urbanist**  geometric, sans-serif, available everywhere, with a wide range of weights and a clean authority that works equally well on screen and in print. It fits the brand's personality and it'll scale without compromise.

The visual identity elements were the hardest to get right. Without a strong visual reference, I tested a wide range of shapes and directions  most felt either too decorative or too generic, either too artistic or too corporate. The break came from looking closely at the logo itself: the stroke forming a rounded square around the mark. The interior shape didn't translate, but that outer form did. I built the entire visual language around it  a rounded-corner rectangle, scaled and proportioned to context, in the primary brand colors. For images and document layouts, I introduced an "L" shape derived from cutting that rectangle along a grid  always anchored to the corners, always structured. *[Images]* Simple enough to reproduce in any software, distinct enough to be recognizable.

For icons, I chose Remix Icons  open, consistent, and available everywhere, for the same reasons as Urbanist. Everything in this system was designed to be tool-agnostic: no special effects, no layered shadows or blurs that break outside of Figma. Anyone on the team should be able to apply it, anywhere.

With all of that resolved, I documented everything into the **NexLed Brand Book**  a reference for anyone creating content under the brand, from product pages to printed materials. *[Link]*

---

With the identity established, the next step was translating it into a UI component library for web use. The challenge here isn't just visual  it's finding the design language that carries the brand's personality into functional, interactive elements without losing fidelity to either.

I followed the visual logic from the brand book directly: the primary button is exactly what it sounds like  a rounded rectangle in primary green. But building components quickly revealed a problem I hadn't anticipated. The palette I'd defined was designed with graphic production in mind, not UI states. Hover, active, visited, disabled  these require color steps that simply didn't exist yet. Rather than force the existing colors into roles they weren't built for, I expanded the system: two lighter greens for hover states and backgrounds, a visited link tone, and three grey values for neutral UI surfaces. Small additions, but they made the difference between a palette and a usable design system.

From there I built out the components the actual projects needed  the store, the support page, the EPREL tool. I deliberately avoided building for hypothetical use cases; every component has a real destination. *[Figma link]*

Both the brand book and the component library are complete in Figma. The current phase is migrating them into two internal web pages  one for brand guidelines, one for the component library with a smoother workflow for copying elements directly into new projects. Final tokenization and consistency passes are still in progress.
