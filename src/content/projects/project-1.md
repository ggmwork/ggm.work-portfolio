        - >-
          When I joined Tec IT, the gap between what the brands were and how
          they appeared was hard to ignore. Tec IT owns several brands 
          *NexLed*, *NexWatt*, *EvVision*, *JG Domótica* and none of them had a
          consistent visual identity, a defined tone of voice, or any system to
          keep communications coherent across touchpoints. 
        - >- 
          Content was being
          created project by project, decision by decision, with nothing to
          anchor it. So i decided that a rebranding was needed, so i started putting together a report analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of each
          brand and the direction I believed each one should take.
        - >-
          When I presented it, my superior's reaction was more guarded than I expected.
          He acknowledged the value but felt a full multi-brand overhaul
          wasn't the priority. I pushed back, arguing that without defined
          visual rules every piece of content we produced was starting from
          zero, and that inconsistency compounds quickly. He recognized it, but
          held his position: the company's focus at the time was NexLed, and with B2C
          expansion on the horizon, that's where the effort should go first.
          I partially agreed. 
        - >-
          Doing all brands at once would have allowed us to
          create distinct personas and a unifying system the smarter long-term
          play. But focusing on one meant moving faster, and faster mattered at
          that moment.

        - >-
          Before touching anything visual, I built the strategic foundation for the rebrand.
          Target audience mapping, tone of voice, brand positioning,
          institutional identity. These documents would make every visual
          decision defensible rather than arbitrary. 
        - >-
          The clearest output of this
          phase was understanding exactly who NexLed needed to speak to and what
          made them worth listening to: a company that designs and manufactures
          custom LED lighting solutions from scratch, built to last, built to
          perform, with a genuine focus on the client's specific need rather
          than a catalogue. 
        - >-
          The insight that drove most of the creative direction came from
          looking at competitors, which tend to fall into one of two traps
          either deeply technical and cold, or aggressively trendy and hollow.
          NexLed needed to occupy the space between those, speaking fluently to
          both industry professionals and end consumers without alienating
          either. Confident enough to be credible, human enough to be
          approachable, and distinct enough to be remembered.

      paragraphs: 
        - >-
          With the strategy defined, it was time to make concrete decisions, but several
          of them came with real constraints.The first one was the logo,do to legal constraints, redesigning it would have required renewing
          its legal registration in Portugal, which wasn't viable at that moment. 
        - >-
          So we made the decision to leave the mark itself untouched and with that constraint in mind, I built a clear set of
          rules about how it relates to the Tec IT brand, how it should
          and shouldn't be presented, and what contexts it belongs in. next i moved to the color palette.
        - >-
          I had no existing reference to build from it, as it hadn't been done before, so for inspiration I went
          back to the logo itself. The gradient running through it  red, green,
          blue  gave me a starting point, and the green tone stood out
          immediately because none of NexLed's main competitors use green, the industry
          defaults to blue and yellow for most brands. Choosing green was both a differentiation
          move and a meaningful one. It connects naturally to the brand's
          sustainability and efficiency positioning, and its darker primary
          shade gives the brand a maturity that lighter palettes don't.
        - >-
          Red and blue became the secondary colors and also complementary neutral
          tones rounding out the system. I also built a set of brighter
          secondary variants for each primary for moments where something
          needs to stand out or feel more alive.

  - component: "ProjTextCardRight"
    props:
      heading: "Typography & Visual Identity"
      paragraphs: 
        - >-
          Typography was a deliberate departure from the expected. Most brands use two families together for there typography. but i wanted to try keep it simple and use only one typeface, but we didn't have a default font. So i started looking for one. 
          The logical starting point would have been the logo's own font.
          But it isn't available on Google Fonts or similar platforms, which
          means importing it manually into every tool: Photoshop, Canva,
          presentations, web, etc. This creates friction at every step, especially as the
          brand grows. In my research i landed on **Urbanist**, a geometric, sans-serif, typeface available everywhere, with a wide range of weights and a clean
          authority that works equally well on screen and in print. I did some tests with different ones but this has the best balance of modernity and readability and the only one that fit the brand's personality.
          <!-- METER AQUI A IMAGEM COM A TIPOGRAFIA -->
        - >-
          The visual identity elements were the hardest to get right because there was no previous visual reference so I tested a wide range of shapes and
          directions. Most felt either too decorative or too generic, either
          too artistic or too corporate. The breakthought came from looking closely at
          the logo itself: the stroke forming a rounded square around the X mark.

        - >-
          The interior shape didn't translate well, but that outer form did. I built
          the entire visual language around this concept, using a rounded-corner rectangle,
          scaled and proportioned to context, in the primary brand colors and also a stroke for the images and document layouts, an "L" shape derived from the logo's stroke, always anchored to the corners.
          Simple enough to reproduce in any software,
          distinct enough to be recognizable.
          <!-- METER AQUI A IMAGEM COM A VISUAL IDENTITY -->

        - >-
          For icons, I chose Remix Icons for the same reasons as Urbanist they are consistent and available
          everywhere. Everything in this system was designed to be tool-agnostic: no special effects, no
          layered shadows or blurs that could be transferred to other tools/media. Anyone on the
          team should be able to apply it anywhere.

        - >-
          The next step was translating it into a
          UI component library for web use. The challenge here is finding the design language that carries the brand's
          personality into functional, interactive elements without losing the visuals.
          In the beginning, I followed the visuals from the brand book directly: the primary
          button is exactly the main shape used in the brand book, a rounded rectangle in primary
          green. 
        - >-
          But building components quickly revealed a problem I hadn't
          anticipated. The palette I'd defined was designed with graphic
          production in mind, not UI states like hover, active, visited, disabled 
          these require colors that didn't exist yet. Rather than
          force the existing colors into roles they weren't built for, I
          expanded the system: two lighter greens for hover states and
          backgrounds, a visited link tone, and three grey values for neutral UI
          surfaces. Small additions, but they made the difference and allowed for a better design system.
        - >-
          From there I built out the components for the projects needed: the
          store, the support page, the EPREL tool. I deliberately avoided
          building for hypothetical use cases, for saving time and effort, every component has a real
          destination. I can check the final result here <!--LINK PARA OS PROJETOS --> and also see some examples in the images below.
